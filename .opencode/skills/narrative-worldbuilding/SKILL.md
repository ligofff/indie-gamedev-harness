# Narrative And Worldbuilding

Narrative should support player action, game tone, and implementation constraints. Preserve a small set of explicit world rules and consequences so new content remains coherent.

## Story And Characters

- Define what characters want, fear, believe, hide, and change. Dialogue should reveal conflict, choice, or voice.
- Outline playable beats as player situation, goal, obstacle, choice, consequence, and changed world state; cut beats that neither alter action nor meaning.
- Keep character voices distinct through priorities, vocabulary, rhythm, and relationship to the player, not gimmick spelling.
- Give factions goals, resources, internal disagreements, and reasons their actions affect the world.
- Use existing project-native lore records, or create them only when demonstrated continuity, complexity, decision, or handoff need warrants it; include canon facts, source, canon level (established, provisional, or under review), confidence, timeline, affected places or factions, player visibility (known, discoverable, hidden), and deliberate contradictions.
- Use an existing project-native contradictions watchlist, or create one only when demonstrated continuity, complexity, decision, or handoff need warrants it; include conflicting claims, intentionality, affected content, owner, and resolution or reveal condition. Do not silently overwrite unresolved lore.
- When ambiguity is intentional, maintain mystery truth privately: answer, clues, reveal conditions, false readings allowed, and facts that must not be contradicted.

## World Rules

- Worldbuilding includes geography, ecology, history, technology or magic limits, culture, economy, and power structures only when they affect play or narrative.
- Keep chronological world timeline: event, cause, consequence, who knows it, when player can learn it, and facts still hidden. Check new scenes against it.
- Give each playable location a premise: purpose, controlling force, daily function, local conflict, visible history, and environmental evidence. Geography, resources, travel, and ecology must explain why people and factions act there.
- Every rule needs consequences. Avoid lore that cannot affect a scene, decision, place, or conflict.
- Integrate narrative with levels: put story information in routes, encounter stakes, objectives, interactables, landmarks, and aftermath; use exposition only when play cannot carry it.
- Use environmental storytelling where interaction and level context can communicate more cheaply than exposition.

## Implementation

- Match dialogue length and complexity to UI, pacing, input, localization, and accessibility limits.
- Constrain player-facing text by reading context: one actionable idea per prompt, plain wording for critical instructions, skippable or pausable delivery where pacing allows, and no vital information solely in optional dialogue.
- Keep player-facing text externalized through the project's text system when one exists.
- Provide context for lines that translators cannot infer: speaker, audience, tone, variables, and state.
- For voiced dialogue, also record line ID, speaker, emotional delivery, scene/action context, listener, timing or interruption limits, subtitle/caption intent, and alternate or fallback line.
- For conditional dialogue, specify entry state, required facts or choices, variable values, state changes, repeat behavior, and fallback line. Do not make progression depend on a line whose state dependency is implicit.
- Review for contradictions, accidental stereotypes, unclear pronouns, inaccessible color-only meaning, and text that blocks play.
